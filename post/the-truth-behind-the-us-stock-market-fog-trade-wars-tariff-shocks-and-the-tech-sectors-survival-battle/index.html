The Truth Behind the U.S. Stock Market Fog: Trade Wars, Tariff Shocks, and the Tech Sector's Survival Battle

2025-04-29 8 min read

I. Introduction: The "Deep V" Puzzle in U.S. Markets

On Monday, April 28, 2025, U.S. markets staged a dramatic "deep V" reversal. Although the major indices ended roughly flat, volatility remained unnervingly high. The semiconductor sector, in particular, struggled. Nvidia — once the undisputed king of AI — once again took center stage for all the wrong reasons, raising doubts about the sustainability of the broader AI narrative.

1. Nvidia: From Deity to Doubt

Nvidia's stock slid toward its monthly moving average before eking out a modest recovery, signaling investor unease about its future. News that Huawei would soon launch a competing AI chip — while unlikely to challenge Nvidia’s dominance in the high-end space — fueled fears that U.S. chip export restrictions could push Chinese buyers away. Whether Nvidia can break above the key resistance zone ($118-$120) now becomes critical.

2. Semiconductors: How Much Life Is Left in the AI Story?

The broader weakness in chips isn't just Nvidia’s problem — it's a referendum on the entire AI-driven market rally. More than one-third of S&P 500 companies have reported Q1 results so far, showing an earnings growth of 9.7% year-over-year, better than the 8% expected in early April. But with looming tariff shocks, whether this growth can continue remains an open question.

II. Trade Wars: The Butterfly Effect on the Global Economy

Last week, Trump told Time magazine he expects to strike trade agreements with countries seeking tariff reductions within three to four weeks. White House Press Secretary Leviitt confirmed talks are ongoing and promised more details soon. However, today, Besent stated that negotiations with China have been shelved for now, with the U.S. prioritizing deals with 15–17 other nations — emphasizing that "China must bow first."

1. Tariff Shock: From Expectation to Reality

The tariff effects are already trickling down. Since April 25, U.S. consumers have started feeling the pain of rising prices. Chinese platforms Temu and Shein announced price hikes — beauty and health products rose an average of 51%, with some categories spiking as much as 377%. On Amazon, early April data showed 930 tracked products with an average price hike of 29%.

2. A "Seismic" Shift in Supply Chains

Apollo Global’s chief economist warned that the U.S. faces a classic stagflation shock, with a full-blown recession likely by summer 2025. Trucking and retail sectors will be among the hardest hit as tariffs wreak havoc on supply chains and consumer behavior.

3. Fed's Rising Alarm: Trade Risks Take Center Stage

According to the Federal Reserve’s latest semiannual Financial Stability Report, a record 73% of respondents now list "global trade risks" as their top concern — more than double the figure from last November. Policy uncertainty is also rapidly climbing the list of market fears.

III. Corporate "Self-Rescue": Buybacks and Earnings Outlook

Despite the turbulence, U.S. corporations are fighting back — with buybacks. Goldman Sachs estimates that about 40% of companies have reopened their buyback windows, and by the end of this week, around 65% will be eligible. Last week's total buyback volume hit $85.8 billion, higher than the seasonal norm.

1. Buyback Surge: A Lifeline for Markets

Goldman expects buyback activity to continue ramping up, peaking next week. Full-year 2025 buyback authorizations are projected to hit a record $1.35 trillion, with actual executed buybacks approaching $1.1 trillion. This massive corporate support is injecting much-needed confidence into markets.

2. Tech Giants Step Into the Spotlight

This week, roughly 41% of S&P 500 companies will report earnings — including heavyweights like Meta, Microsoft, Amazon, and Apple. Analysts expect them to reaffirm their AI investment commitments, which could offer a near-term tailwind for companies like Nvidia.

IV. Bitcoin and Deep-Sea Mining: Alternative Market Narratives

While traditional equities wobble, alternative assets are telling a different story. MicroStrategy’s (MSTR) stock has soared alongside Bitcoin’s resurgence. Interestingly, Google searches for "Bitcoin" remain at historic lows, suggesting that institutional, rather than retail, buyers are driving the rally.

1. Bitcoin: A Shift From Retail to Institutional Investors

Publicly traded companies now hold 705,373 bitcoins — about 3.35% of total supply. This indicates a fundamental shift: Bitcoin’s recent strength is fueled by institutions, advisors, corporations, and even governments, rather than the typical retail investor crowd. The market structure is evolving.

2. Deep-Sea Mining: America's Strategic Push

Trump had earlier signed an executive order accelerating deep-sea mining permits, aiming to reduce U.S. dependence on China’s dominance in critical minerals. With China recently tightening rare earth exports, pressure is mounting for America to fast-track domestic mining initiatives.

V. Consumer Weakness: The Domino’s Pizza Warning Sign

Domino’s Q1 report revealed a 0.5% decline in same-store U.S. sales, missing analyst expectations for a 0.5% gain. Traffic slowed noticeably in February and March, as lower-income groups tightened discretionary spending, while margins were hit by higher food costs.

1. Consumer Strain: Early Signs of Recession

While Domino’s managed to slightly beat on revenue and profit, it issued cautious guidance, warning that persistent economic uncertainty could derail its full-year targets. The broader takeaway: consumer weakness is real — and may worsen across more sectors in the coming months.

2. Domino’s Defies Gravity: But For How Long?

Despite sector-wide struggles, Domino’s shares have soared 17% year-to-date, far outperforming the broader market. The outperformance likely stems from its strong international business, but questions remain about whether this resilience can last.

VI. Conclusion: Where Does the U.S. Market Go From Here?

The market seems torn between short-term optimism and longer-term worries: corporate buybacks and tech earnings are providing support, but trade wars, tariff shocks, and weak consumption are clear drags. Monday’s S&P 500 daily candle formed a doji — a classic sign of indecision.

1. Short-Term Bounce vs. Long-Term Decline

JPMorgan’s trading desk has turned tactically bullish, expecting strong tech earnings and positive trade developments to fuel further gains after the recent pullback. However, they caution that this rally is mostly technical, not fundamental. The S&P could climb higher in the coming weeks but may ultimately retrace to the 5100–5500 range.

2. Fed's Dilemma: Fiscal Sustainability Risks Rising

In its latest Financial Stability Report, the Fed noted that "U.S. government debt sustainability" has fallen to only the third biggest concern. Yet if tariffs trigger a major sell-off, shrinking capital gains taxes could sharply reduce government revenue — worsening fiscal stress.

3. Bitcoin's Emerging Opportunity

The U.S.-led trade conflict could force more global money printing to cushion the blow, setting the stage for Bitcoin to outperform. Additionally, a weakening dollar could drive further decoupling between Bitcoin and traditional tech stocks.

Final Thoughts: A Market at a Crossroads

Markets now stand at a critical crossroads: short-term optimism fueled by buybacks and earnings versus long-term risks from tariffs, trade wars, and weakening consumer demand. Over the next few months, how these forces interact will define the ultimate direction. Investors must stay nimble — ready to seize short-term opportunities while preparing for long-term turbulence.
