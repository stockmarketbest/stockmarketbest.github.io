Trump Tweets Shake Markets: Dissecting the Unflappable Bull’s Illusions

2025-04-23 9 min read

Introduction: Market Euphoria or Illusion?

On April 22, 2025, major U.S. equity indices surged more than 2.5%, with every sector in the green. Pre-market earnings beats, stabilization in U.S. Treasuries and the dollar, and a flood of liquidity all pointed to a flawless rally. But was this truly a “spring awakening,” or merely another smokescreen engineered by former President Trump’s tweets? Are investors genuinely rescued, or simply dancing to political rhetoric? This report peels back the layers to expose the market’s true complexion—and shows how to survive this mirage.

I. Earnings Mirage: Is 3M the Lifeline?

1.1 3M’s Report: Economic Bellwether or Joke?

Industrial conglomerate 3M topped expectations, sending its shares sharply higher. Wall Street hailed it as an economic barometer—after all, 3M’s footprint spans consumer and industrial markets. Management reaffirmed its 2025 adjusted EPS guidance of $7.60–$7.90, easing investor concerns.

Yet can a company worth under $100 billion truly represent the economy? Annual U.S. tariffs alone have cost 3M roughly $850 million—knocking $0.20–$0.40 off EPS. In reality, 3M was a convenient prop to distract from yesterday’s declines. The market simply needed a “hero,” and 3M was drafted onto the stage.

1.2 The Bounce: Real Liquidity or Fickle Sentiment?

Global markets reopened after Easter, unleashing pent-up cash and fueling demand. But is this genuine, long-term liquidity, or just short-term “hot money” playing musical chairs? Market sentiment remains more fickle than any presidential tweet—one misstep, and retail investors risk becoming the ultimate bag-holders.

II. Trade Talks: Trump’s Rhetorical Theater

2.1 India, Japan, Mexico: All Smoke, No Fire

U.S. Trade Representative signals “major progress” after Vice President Pence met India’s Prime Minister Modi; Bank of America’s C-suites predict tariffs with China will ease soon. But Friday’s boasts about Japan fell flat when Tokyo denied any breakthrough; yesterday’s praise for talks with Mexico was contradicted by President Andrés Manuel López Obrador’s office, which reported no consensus. This isn’t negotiation—it’s a White House one-man show.

2.2 China’s Warning: Retaliation Armed and Ready

A White House faction has tried to rally allies to isolate China. Beijing swiftly retorted: any country siding with the U.S. should “brace for retaliation.” While the Trump administration fans the flames of trade conflict, global investors grow ever more jittery.

2.3 Tweet Power: Misinformation Everywhere

With every official statement trumpeting “progress” and “great deals,” not a single detail is released. Investors are left parsing Trump’s tweets, turning the market into an insiders’ playground. Retail investors chase highs blindly while institutions quietly trim positions. The S&P 500 now oscillates between 5,000 and 5,500—directionless and unmoored.

III. Trump’s Tweets: The Ultimate Market Driver

3.1 Retail’s Nightmare: Day Trades or Total Loss

A single tweet can trigger violent swings, forcing retail traders into rapid churning. Lacking access to insider scoops, they’re doomed to lag—and often end up wiped out.

3.2 Institutional Tactics: De-risk and Observe

Fearing Trump’s next 280-character bombshell, institutions have offloaded positions, refusing to wade back in until clarity returns. In choppy ranges, they execute support-sell and quick-in-quick-out strategies—tools most retail players simply don’t possess.

3.3 Long-Term Escape: The Retail Survival Guide

Rather than follow each tweet, investors should adopt a “Buddhist” patience. Quality stocks and ETFs trading at depressed levels—such as Walmart, Lockheed Martin (LMT), JPMorgan Chase (JPM), Disney (DIS), and the Nasdaq-100 ETF (QQQ)—offer multi-year value. Buy and hold for three to five years; political theatrics won’t shake fundamental strength.

IV. Market Outlook: UBS Pours Cold Water—and Offers Hope

4.1 No Trough Yet: One More Leg Down

UBS strategists warn stocks haven’t bottomed. Earnings forecasts may be trimmed, the Fed remains slow to respond, and valuations are still lofty. Bottom-picking now is premature; real support may only emerge in Q3 when credit spreads normalize and rate cuts begin.

4.2 Rebound Scenarios: 5,300 or 5,500?

Analysts project the S&P 500 could rally to 5,300 by year-end; if U.S.–China tariffs halve, 5,500 isn’t out of the question. But don’t bank on a full-year gain—expensive valuations make a close in the red more likely.

4.3 The Annual Barometer: 4,835 as Bedrock?

April 7’s low of 4,835 may prove the annual floor. Historically, Fed tightening couldn’t breach the five-year moving average; now, with easing on the horizon and AI adoption accelerating, a true recessionary shock would be needed to crack this level.

V. Sector Spotlight: The Blood and Tears Behind Earnings

5.1 Tesla: Can a Halved Valuation Survive?

Q1 revenue of $19.34 billion (vs. $21.37 billion expected) and EPS of $0.27 (vs. $0.43) disappointed; free cash flow came in at $664 million (vs. $1.08 billion). Tariffs weighed on auto sales, yet the stock barely budged—the bad news was fully priced in. Strength in energy storage and the imminent launch of an affordable model in early 2025 offer a silver lining.

5.2 NVIDIA: Rumors Run Wild

After a slight rebound, yesterday’s report that Amazon postponed data-center orders was swiftly refuted. Speculation of a U.S.–China AI-chip partnership remains in limbo without Trump’s blessing. The AI boom rages on—policy risk, however, keeps it tethered.

5.3 Defence Stocks: Icy Lows, Fiery Highs

  • Northrop Grumman (NOC): EPS plunged 49% as B-21 costs spiraled, sending shares down 12.6%; tariffs further squeeze margins, likely keeping it near $425.
  • Lockheed Martin (LMT): Stellar results, $173 billion in orders, and firm support at its 50-month moving average make it a standout long-term pick.
  • Raytheon Technologies (RTX): Ahead on revenue and earnings, but tariffs clipped shares by nearly 10% in a late-stage profit-taking move.

VI. Bitcoin: A Decoupling Dawn

6.1 $90,000 Record: A New Safe Haven?

Bitcoin vaulted past $90,000 as crypto names rallied. Investors sense a shift: it’s no longer tethered to tech stocks but reclaiming its role as “digital gold,” signaling a real decoupling from U.S. assets.

6.2 Return to Decentralization

Originally designed as a stateless currency, Bitcoin had been drawn into the Nasdaq’s orbit by funds. Its renewed status as a true hedge is a major plus for retail participants.

VII. Retail Strategy: How Not to Get Sliced and Diced

7.1 The Day-Trade Dead End

Speed alone won’t outpace insiders. Avoid playing tag with tweets—retail traders simply lack the edge.

7.2 The Long Game: Conviction Wins

Identify high-quality assets, log off social media, and hold. Walmart’s stability, LMT’s order backlog, JPM’s financial dominance, DIS’s enduring franchises, and QQQ’s tech exposure are three-to-five-year winners that won’t budge under political noise.

7.3 Technical Guardrails: The Five-Year Moving Average

Use the annual chart’s 4,835 level as your lifeline: hold above it, stay invested; dip below, consider stops. In choppy ranges, buy at support, sell near resistance, and let time do the heavy lifting.

Conclusion: Trump’s Tweets as Psychological Warfare

Trump’s tweets are not economic signals but weapons of sentiment. Every headline is calculated; every swing, a trap. To thrive in this “impregnable” market, investors must divorce themselves from emotion, anchor portfolios in sound fundamentals, and let technicals steer risk. In 2025, chaos is the norm—and clarity remains the rarest commodity.
