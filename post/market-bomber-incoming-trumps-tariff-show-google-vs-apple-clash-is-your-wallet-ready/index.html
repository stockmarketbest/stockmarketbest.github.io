Market Bomber Incoming: Trump’s Tariff Show, Google vs. Apple Clash—Is Your Wallet Ready?

2025-05-09 7 min read

A Seasonal Sniffle or a Market Malady?

Hello, everyone, and welcome back to my corner! I’ve been under the weather these past couple of days—must be the changing seasons messing with my immune system, turning me into a roasted chestnut with no energy. Have you felt this too? Ever since the pandemic and those vaccine shots, every cold feels like it’s been turbocharged. My limbs feel like lead, and I’m “frozen” in place—but the markets wait for no one. Let’s push through and talk.

1. Market Noise, Deafening

Lately, the market has become a non-stop “noise bomber.” First, former President Trump flexes his tariff muscles—dropping semiconductor incentives one moment, then signing a trade agreement with the UK the next (details? Maybe in a few weeks). Meanwhile, Google and Apple are at it again: Apple threatens to switch Safari’s default engine back to its own, and Google’s stock immediately took a dive. Flashy headlines, but when you dig deeper, it’s all volume and no substance.

1.1 Trump’s Tariff “Gimmicks”

Trump treats tariffs like party tricks—one minute he’s raising them, the next he’s backing off, and the market rides the roller coaster. “Semiconductor support”? Just political smoke and mirrors. The UK trade deal? Let’s be real: since Brexit, the UK economy isn’t the “Great British Empire” it once was. This pact is pure face-saving. Rumors of tariffs dropping to 50% are just thunder without rain—every day a new twist. Investors who dance to his tune risk stepping into a trap. Frankly, he can’t help himself; he must stir the pot to prove he’s “got game.” But for your portfolio? It’s practically irrelevant.

1.2 Google vs. Apple “Search Wars”

Then there’s the latest drama between Google and Apple. Apple’s promise to drop Google as Safari’s default search engine sent Google’s shares skidding. But is it really that damaging? Anyone who’s used Safari knows its search is like a relic from the last century—long overdue for the scrap heap. Google, flush with cash and boasting cutting-edge AI, just announced a $7 billion share buyback. Apple’s move feels more like clickbait—designed to spook retail investors. Yes, AI search is emerging as a competitor, but Google won’t sit by idly. This is a teapot tempest, unlikely to create a tidal wave.

2. The Fed’s “Talk Show”

The Fed’s FOMC meeting also grabbed headlines. Chair Powell rattled on, holding rates steady—essentially saying nothing at all. This gathering was pure “talk show,” empty of substance. Powell isn’t a showman like Trump; he speaks cautiously, leaving investors with virtually zero new insights. He did note that U.S. first-quarter data remain strong, with both the labor market and consumer spending intact—a point worth acknowledging. The U.S. economy hasn’t collapsed yet; life goes on.

2.1 Strong Economy—Don’t Pop the Champagne Yet

Robust data sound encouraging, but don’t break out the bubbly prematurely. The labor market and consumption may hold firm, but the inflation beast isn’t fully tamed. Powell’s silence signals ongoing caution at the Fed. A resilient economy is good, but too much strength can hide risks—if inflation resurges, interest rates could climb again, and that would rattle the stock market.

3. Rate Cuts? Dream On!

Many are hoping for a Fed rate cut as a “lifeline” for stocks. I’ll pour cold water on that: the reason for a rate cut matters as much as the cut itself. An ideal scenario is a stable economy with easing inflation, allowing the Fed to cut rates and ignite a rally—like in 2019. But if rate cuts stem from rising unemployment and a faltering economy, that’s not good news—it’s a red flag. Stocks might tank instead of rising, a bloodbath rather than a boon. So, don’t cheer a rate cut blindly; understand why it’s happening, or you may regret it.

3.1 Market Patterns: Highs Followed by Lows?

The S&P 500 and Nasdaq 100 have enjoyed a 20% rally, tempting though it is. But remember: big gains often invite sharp pullbacks. Today’s doji candle on the S&P near its 200-day moving average suggests pressure. I’d expect a 5–7% correction—just a pause after that 20% surge. But fear not: I don’t see a repeat of April’s lows. With Trump easing tariffs slightly, NVIDIA even jumped to $117 today, showing the market’s sensitivity. Short-term volatility is inevitable, but a long-term crash? Unlikely.

4. Investment Tip—Power Down

After all that, my advice is blunt: pick solid core assets, then turn off the news and trading apps. The market invents noise daily, but 99% of it is meaningless; watching it only breeds anxiety. You win today, you panic tomorrow, and in the end your emotions tank while your portfolio stagnates. Someone asked me recently, “Is daily review useful?” My honest answer: not really. My videos are for entertainment, not daily trading playbooks. For real investing, obsessing over headlines does nothing. Stay calm, ignore the static, and you’ll be fine.

4.1 Noise as “Mental Pollution”

Market noise is nothing more than “mental pollution.” Trump hollers, Google and Apple feud, media amplifies—and retail investors overthink and overtrade. The result? Meager gains and a surplus of anxiety. Hold quality assets, block out distractions, sip your tea, stroll your dog, and watch your life improve—returns will follow.

4.2 Review Sessions as “Smoke and Mirrors”

Daily review sessions are a comfort blanket for the anxious. My videos meet that demand, but for investing, obsessing over trivial headlines is like asking a doctor “How am I?” every 20 minutes. The real skill is filtering information, focusing on macro trends, and ignoring the “smoke and mirrors.”

5. Conclusion: Defend Your Wallet

To summarize: market noise abounds but adds no value; the Fed’s meeting was hollow; the U.S. economy is strong but not invincible; rate cuts require context; short-term corrections are normal, but a long-term crash is unlikely. The investment mantra: choose good stocks, turn off the screen, and live your life.
