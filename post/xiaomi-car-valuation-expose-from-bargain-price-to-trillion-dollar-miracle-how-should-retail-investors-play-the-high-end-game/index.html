Xiaomi Car Valuation Exposé: From “Bargain Price” to “Trillion-Dollar Miracle” – How Should Retail Investors Play the High-End Game?

2025-03-09 9 min read

# xiaomi su7 ultra # Xiaomi Car # stock market

I. Xiaomi’s Car-Making Journey: From “Bargain Prices” to “Golden Tags”

1.1 A Stock Price Surge: The Awakening of Retail Investors and a Capital Carnival

Just a year ago, Xiaomi’s shares were languishing around “HK$20 bargain-bin” levels, barely noticed by anyone. Today, they’ve transformed into “HK$50 prime-time” stocks, propelling the company’s market value toward the trillion-dollar club. This dramatic shift begs the question: Will Xiaomi’s cars take flight, or is it just the boundless imagination of retail investors at work?

  • The HK$20 Era: Retail investors barely gave Xiaomi a second glance—as if it were just another company selling phone cases.
  • The HK$50 Era: Suddenly, these same investors discovered, “Hey, this car actually has four wheels!” and rushed to buy in, fearing they might miss out on the “next Tesla.”

1.2 The Sales Secret: A 300,000-Vehicle Target – Is It All Talk or a Sweet Deal?

Xiaomi has boldly declared a target of selling 300,000 cars by 2025, and the first two months have already shown “20,000+ units per month.” On the surface, the yearly goal appears to be a piece of cake. Yet, on closer inspection:

  • From 135,000 to 300,000: What will drive such a doubling in sales? Is it a dramatic expansion in production capacity, or are consumers collectively suffering from a “Xiaomi faith syndrome”?
  • The SU7 Pricing Puzzle: With prices ranging between HK$210,000 and HK$290,000, the midpoint of HK$250,000 seems like a math teacher’s dream. However, with Tesla cutting prices next door and BYD causing a stir, is Xiaomi positioning itself as a high-end brand or merely exuding an inexplicable confidence?

II. The Price-to-Earnings Rumble: Tesla, Volkswagen, Li Auto – Who’s the Real Valuation Powerhouse?

2.1 Measuring Up to Tesla: Where Does a 128x PE Ratio’s “Money-Making Power” Come From?

Using Tesla’s valuation logic, Xiaomi’s automotive business could be worth as much as HK$500 billion. Yet, this comparison raises a burning question: Tesla’s PE is based on tech stock expectations—could Xiaomi’s PE be nothing more than a “PowerPoint stock”?

  • Tesla’s Magic Formula: Selling cars is just a side hustle; it’s Full Self-Driving (FSD) that really prints money, boasting software margins as high as 70%. Hardware? It’s merely there as a friendly accessory!
  • Xiaomi’s Imitation Game: CEO Lei Jun claims that hardware margins will not exceed 5%, as the company attempts to mimic Tesla’s software-driven profits. But there’s a catch—Xiaomi’s autonomous driving is still in its “learner’s permit” phase. Would consumers really pay for a technology that’s still in driving-school mode?

2.2 Volkswagen vs. Li Auto: “Tortoise-Like” PEs for Traditional Automakers Versus “Roller-Coaster Valuations” for New Players

  • Volkswagen: The “Old Hand” with a 5x PE: With a market share as steady as a mountain, its growth is slower than Beijing’s third ring road at rush hour. The capital market’s valuation is akin to giving a pensioner a token bonus—just a modest nod.
  • Li Auto: The “New Money” with a 22x PE: Although its sales are booming, the market whispers, “Is your extended-range system a breakthrough or just a quick fix exploiting policy loopholes?”

2.3 Xiaomi’s Valuation Conundrum: HK$500 Billion or HK$20 Billion? It All Depends on Whether the Market Believes the Hype

  • If Compared to Tesla: A profit of HK$3.75 billion multiplied by a 128x PE equals HK$480 billion, prompting retail investors to cheer, “Xiaomi is destined to be the Eastern Tesla!”
  • If Compared to Volkswagen: HK$3.75 billion times a 5x PE equals HK$19 billion, leaving shareholders figuratively free-falling from a rooftop.
  • The Only Truth: Valuation is never just a math problem—it’s a game of “Mafia” in the capital markets. Reveal your hand too soon, and you might just get cut down.

III. Breaking Down Xiaomi’s “Trillion-Dollar Trump Card”: Hardware as a Friend, Software as a Money Grab?

3.1 A 5% Hardware Margin: A Promise of Integrity or a Business Trap?

When Lei Jun promises that hardware margins won’t exceed 5%, it sounds as if he’s saying, “We won’t make big money off you—we’re just here for the fun.” But a deeper look reveals:

  • The Old Mobile Phone Trick: Xiaomi’s phones have long hovered below a 5% margin, yet they rake in billions annually from advertising and an ecosystem of services. Could its car business simply be “Phone 2.0”?
  • A Word of Caution: If the software side fails to turn a profit while hardware struggles, Xiaomi’s automotive venture could become nothing more than a “charity car project.”

3.2 Software Monetization: The “Pie-in-the-Sky” Approach of Autonomous Driving

Tesla rakes in tens of billions annually from its FSD, and Xiaomi hopes to snag a slice of that pie. However, the reality is stark:

  • A Technological Gap: Tesla’s autonomous driving mileage outstrips Xiaomi’s by a factor of 100—like comparing a PhD thesis to a primary school essay.
  • Consumer Perception: While consumers are willing to pay for Elon Musk’s “Mars dream,” will they open their wallets for Lei Jun’s “value-for-money autonomous driving”?

IV. The Ultimate Question: What Justifies Comparing Xiaomi Cars to Tesla?

4.1 The Ecosystem Bet: Mobile Phones + Cars + AIoT – A Closed Loop or a Dead End?

Xiaomi’s ace in the hole is its “ecosystem synergy,” yet a car isn’t a smart bracelet. When buying a vehicle, do consumers really expect it to seamlessly sync with their Xiaomi air conditioners?

4.2 The Value Proposition Dilemma: The Impossible Triangle of High-End Positioning

The Xiaomi SU7 is priced at HK$250,000—it must pack premium components (think CATL batteries and NVIDIA chips), maintain a ≤5% profit margin, and still deliver a high-end image. It’s like expecting one person to be Einstein, Steve Jobs, and a top-tier influencer all at once.

4.3 The Unwritten Rules of Capital Games: Valuation ≠ Value, Belief ≈ Bubble

Tesla’s lofty PE is built on expectations of disrupting traditional industries, while Xiaomi’s current narrative resembles a “Chinese imitation of Tesla.” When market patience runs out, a trillion-dollar valuation might quickly evaporate into mere paper value.

V. A Survival Guide for Retail Investors: Is Xiaomi Car a “Bet It All” Opportunity?

5.1 The Optimists: Xiaomi as the Next Tesla – Missing Out Now Could Mean Losing Out on Ten Bitcoins

  • The Logic: China’s supply chain advantage + a loyal base of “Mi fans” + Lei Jun’s hardworking aura = an unbeatable combination.
  • The Risk: Should Tesla suddenly slash prices to HK$200,000, the Xiaomi SU7 might just end up as the “best runner-up of the year.”

5.2 The Pessimists: A Trillion-Dollar Valuation is a Bubble, and Xiaomi’s Car Is Headed for a Crash

  • The Logic: With a 90% failure rate among new automakers and Xiaomi lacking core technology, its valuation relies entirely on “imaginative PowerPoint presentations.”
  • The Wry Comment: “I used to think Jia Yueting was a scammer; now it seems he just started ten years too early.”

5.3 The Realists: In the Short Term, It’s All About Sentiment; In the Long Run, It’s About Technology

  • The Verdict: If you believe Xiaomi can crack the code on autonomous driving and build a robust software ecosystem, now might be the time to buy the dip. But if you’re only in it for a gamble, you might as well go seek a fortune teller’s advice.

VI. In Conclusion: Is Xiaomi’s Trillion-Dollar Gamble a Myth or a Farce?

The capital markets are never short on stories—they only lack the people who can make those stories a reality. Xiaomi’s valuation frenzy is, at its core, a capital game of “belief or disbelief.” When the tide recedes, one thing will be clear:

  • Tesla’s moat is built on technological dominance,
  • Xiaomi’s moat might just be Lei Jun’s uncanny PowerPoint prowess.

And as for the retail investors? Remember this hard truth: The moment you start believing “this time it’s different,” the reaper’s already raised his scythe.
