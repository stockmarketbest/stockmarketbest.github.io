The Darkest Hour Before the Dawn in U.S. Markets: Tech Stock Collapse and Tariff Warfare Spark a Fierce Battle

2025-04-01 12 min read

Introduction: The Market on the Edge

On March 31, U.S. markets experienced a roller-coaster close to the first quarter. At the open, futures plummeted and star tech stocks hemorrhaged, as if the market had reached rock bottom. Yet within just 15 minutes, the scene shifted dramatically: the S&P 500 rebounded from a 1.6% drop to close up 0.55%, and the Dow surged 1%. This was no miracle but a glimpse into the deeper market logic and institutional maneuvering. Uncertainty over tariff policies looms like a sword of Damocles, while a trust crisis in tech stocks resembles a ticking time bomb. Is this the last gasp before the bull market ends, or merely a prelude to another round of corrections? This report unpacks the layers behind this market melee.

I. Market Dynamics: A Moment of Terror at the Eye of the Storm

1.1 Tariff Policy: A Ticking Global Bomb

The Trump administration’s tariff policy has been likened to a nuclear bomb poised to detonate at any moment, shaking the nerves of global markets. The administration declared that its upcoming "reciprocal tariffs" would no longer be limited to the 10 to 15 countries with the largest trade deficits but would target all nations. Reports indicate that Trump has been pressuring his advisors to adopt a tougher stance on tariffs. This aggressive posture ignited a flight-to-safety reaction, causing futures to collapse pre-market and tech stocks to tumble dramatically. The uncertainty surrounding tariffs not only affects domestic companies but also places the entire global supply chain at risk—triggering soaring costs and compressed profit margins from raw materials to consumer goods.

1.2 Intra-Day Movements: A 15-Minute Life-or-Death Reversal

The day began in panic as the S&P 500 fell 1.6% and the Nasdaq dropped over 2.6% in the early minutes. However, just 15 minutes later, the decline halted abruptly, and the market staged a remarkable reversal. By 2:00 PM, a flood of buying orders pushed the S&P 500 to finish 0.55% higher, while the Dow surged 1%. The key drivers behind this rebound included seasonal rebalancing from pension funds and the extension of JPMorgan Chase’s Collar options strategy. Yet this rebound resembled a well-orchestrated Wall Street performance—a "fake breakdown and genuine liquidation" maneuver. A stop-loss trigger around the 5500-point level forced retail investors out, while institutions stepped in at lower levels and later pushed prices higher.

1.3 Technical Analysis: The Last Line of Defense

The weekly chart of the S&P 500 had touched a long-standing upward trend line—previously a key support level in April and August of the prior year. This breach activated programmed buying signals and forced short sellers to cover, thus driving the intra-day rebound. However, this trend line is not infallible; repeated tests might eventually erode its support. Although the daily chart closed with a long bullish candlestick, this strong signal may only be a temporary boost fueled by short-term funds rather than a sign of genuine market bottoming. History shows that initial rebounds are often followed by a second dip, indicating that today's rally might just be the market catching its breath before another test.

II. Market Sentiment and Institutional Maneuvering: The Hidden War Behind the Scenes

2.1 Pension Fund Rebalancing: A Lifeline or a Poisoned Cure?

As the quarter ended, pension funds rebalanced their portfolios, acting as a temporary painkiller to ease the market's suffering. This influx of funds aimed to adjust asset allocations and manage risk exposure, providing some support amid the downturn. However, this measure is merely a short-term fix. Pension fund buying cannot reverse the market trend but rather offers institutions a chance to accumulate at lower levels. Once this buying pressure subsides, deeper vulnerabilities could be exposed.

2.2 JPMorgan’s Options Strategy: Wall Street’s Masterstroke

JPMorgan’s Collar options strategy played a pivotal role in the rebound. By selling call options at a high strike price, buying put options at a lower strike, and selling even lower-priced puts, the strategy capped upward potential while offering support during declines. Historical data show that this approach has helped stabilize the market on several occasions in April, August of the previous year, and again in March. Yet this double-edged sword carries inherent risks—a total reversal in market sentiment at options expiration could trigger a cascade of selling and exacerbate volatility. Wall Street appears to be playing a high-stakes game, leaving retail investors to fend for themselves.

2.3 Short Squeezes: True Bottoming or a Mirage?

Short-covering added fuel to the intra-day rebound. Once the S&P 500 hit its trend line, programmed buying surged, forcing shorts to cover their positions rapidly. Nevertheless, establishing a true market bottom is rarely instantaneous, and a V-shaped recovery remains an elusive hope. Historical trends indicate that the first low is often retested in the ensuing weeks, suggesting that today's rebound may well be the prelude to a second dip.

III. The Tech Stock Crisis: A Prelude to the Burst of the AI Bubble

3.1 AI Investment: A Costly Illusion

Over the past two years, the AI boom drove tech stock valuations to stratospheric heights. Now, the market is awakening to the reality that the returns on AI investments have not met expectations. Tech giants have poured heavily into AI infrastructure, with capital expenditures eating into profits and free cash flow declining sharply. The “Big Seven” tech index retreated by more than 20% from its peak, slipping into a technical bear market. Confidence in tech stocks evaporated overnight, as AI became seen not as a savior but as a profit-draining black hole.

3.1.1 The Giants in Trouble

NVIDIA, for instance, saw intraday losses of over 5% and closed down 1.18%, having traded within a 100-150 range for nearly 10 months—a sign of waning investor confidence. Tesla opened with a 7.5% drop and closed 1.67% lower, with upcoming Q1 delivery figures expected to decline by 8.5% year-on-year and over 20% sequentially; any miss on these numbers could further exacerbate its decline. AMD too found itself besieged by short positions, as institutional sentiment on its AI prospects turned sharply negative.

3.1.2 The Industry’s Winter

As the AI fervor subsided, the valuation bubble in tech stocks was brutally burst. A report from Morgan Stanley highlighted that hedge funds’ exposure to AI had dropped to a five-year low, underscoring the risks inherent in concentrated institutional positions. In an era of deleveraging, tech stocks became the prime targets for selling, with market sentiment shifting from feverish optimism to cold disillusionment.

3.2 Institutional Sell-Off: The Spark to the Avalanche

Heavy institutional positions in tech giants have made these stocks vulnerable to any shift in market sentiment. NVIDIA, AMD, and Tesla now rank among the most shorted stocks. Although Bitcoin mining firm HUT saw a 23% pre-market surge due to news of an association with Trump’s younger son, its momentum quickly faded, reflecting broader investor disinterest in the “Trump concept.” Moderna, meanwhile, slumped 8.9% amid a wave of resignations among top FDA officials, dragging down vaccine and gene-therapy sectors. The tech winter appears to have set in comprehensively.

3.3 Crisis of Confidence: The Doomsday of Tech Stocks?

High costs and low returns from AI investments have shattered market trust in tech stocks. Coupled with institutional deleveraging and aggressive short positions, the adjustment in tech stocks is far from over. The Nasdaq fell 7.7% in March, with quarterly losses exceeding 8%—its worst performance since Q2 2022. This isn’t just a short-term blip, but the beginning of a fundamental reevaluation of tech stock valuations.

IV. Future Outlook: Where Will the Market Go?

4.1 The Tariff Decision: A Double-Edged Sword

On April 2, the final details of the tariff policy will be unveiled. The market has already factored in a modest 10% rise in anticipation. If the outcome meets expectations, this “boot coming down” could trigger a short-term rebound; however, if Trump opts for an unexpectedly harsh policy, it could spark panic selling due to mispriced risk. Institutions are currently adopting a wait-and-see approach, reducing both long and short exposures to minimal levels. The ultimate impact of tariffs will depend not just on the rates, but on how they reshape global supply chains.

4.2 Non-Farm Payroll Data: The Economic Litmus Test

The non-farm payroll report on April 4 will emerge as a new focal point for the market. This report is expected to reveal whether the U.S. economy is steering towards a soft landing. Compared to tariffs, these employment figures will offer more direct guidance on April’s market trajectory. If the job data comes in weak, panic could intensify; if it surprises on the upside, it might restore some market confidence. Wall Street views the current correction as a “buyable pullback” within an ongoing bull market, but acknowledges that significant challenges lie ahead.

4.3 Institutional Forecasts: A Cascade of Downgrades

Goldman Sachs downgraded its S&P 500 target twice this month—from 6500 to 6200 and then to 5700 points—citing slowing economic growth and a revaluation of market metrics. JPMorgan Chase warned that trade uncertainties could persist into Q2 and Q3, with potential corrections of up to 15% even in the absence of a recession. Deutsche Bank predicted that once short positions are exhausted, the market might need to fall to around 5250 points—a 6% decline from the current close.

4.3.1 JPMorgan’s Strategic Positioning

JPMorgan’s Q2 Collar strategy is already in place: selling call options at 5880, buying put options at 5290, and selling even lower puts at 4460. This setup locks in gains during upward movements and provides support in downturns. The 5290 level is seen as a critical protection threshold—if the index falls below 5500, it could prove to be a make-or-break point.

4.3.2 The Market’s Safety Net

Both Deutsche Bank and Bank of America see the 5250–5290 point range as the market’s potential bottom. Should the index breach these levels, extreme positioning might trigger a reversal. However, more pain is likely to be endured before any sustained recovery sets in.

V. Conclusion: Is There a Glimmer of Hope in the Darkest Hour?

The U.S. stock market currently teeters on the brink, caught between the uncertainties of a looming tariff storm and the collapse of tech stock confidence. The fragile support around the S&P 500 is in question, and the crisis of confidence in tech stocks suggests further adjustments ahead. In the coming days, the final tariff decision and non-farm payroll data will likely determine the market’s fate. Institutional forecasts indicate that pressure will continue through Q2 and Q3, with potential corrections reaching as much as 15%. Investors should monitor the critical 5290 point level closely and manage risks prudently, searching for that first hint of dawn in this dark hour.
