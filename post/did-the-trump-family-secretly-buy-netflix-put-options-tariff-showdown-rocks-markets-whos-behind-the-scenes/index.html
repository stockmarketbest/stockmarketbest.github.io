Did the Trump Family Secretly Buy Netflix “Put” Options? Tariff Showdown Rocks Markets—Who’s Behind the Scenes?

2025-05-06 10 min read

# QQQ # SPY # DIA # .DJI # .SPX # .IXIC # GLD # BTC # TLT # trump # stock market # NVDA # TSLA # AAPL # META # GOOGL # MSFT # AMZN

It’s Monday, May 5, 2025, and as the markets opened, a new cloud of tariff threats hit like a ton of bricks. Over the weekend, Trump didn’t rest—on Sunday he announced a 100% “punitive tariff” on foreign film content and said he has no plans to sit down for casual talks with Xi Jinping. The moment that news broke, Netflix shares plunged over 4% at the open—a real-life “Netflix’s stock cascades thousands of feet.” But then the White House backtracked, clarifying that the film tariff hasn’t been finalized and that Trump still plans to meet with Hollywood execs. Netflix quickly caught its breath and bounced right back. This dramatic reversal is more gripping than one of Netflix’s own Korean dramas, and the market is wondering: did the Trump family quietly buy Netflix put options, ready to cash in on the rebound?

1. Tariff Tactics Gone Wild: From Goods to Services, Trump’s “All-Out” Strategy

Trump is truly breaking the mold this time, swinging the tariff hammer from physical goods straight into the service sector. Slapping tariffs on movies is a “brainstorm” move—targeting not just Netflix and Disney+, but reminding everyone: no industry is safe from Trump’s tariff magic.

1.1 Overseas Netflix Hits Face the Chopping Block: Profits at Risk

Netflix has won global fans with hits like Squid Game, Black Knight, and Three-Body Problem, sending its stock soaring as investors piled in like it was Chinese New Year. Then Trump’s 100% tariff threat lands, doubling costs for foreign content and squeezing profit margins tighter than a rush-hour subway. Investors can’t help but imagine: if this tariff sticks, Netflix’s optimistic guidance could easily tank. Some even suspect the Trump family placed put bets in advance, waiting to profit off the crash. After all, with Netflix’s recent meteoric rise, it’s prime “fat meat” for short sellers.

1.2 Streaming Giants Shake: Disney, Amazon Won’t Escape

Netflix isn’t alone. Disney and Amazon, heavily reliant on overseas production, also got hit. Their stocks fell, vaporizing market value faster than an ice pop on a hot day. Their earnings calls mention tariffs only vaguely—clearly they’re wary of angering the White House, acting like quails in the headlights. Amazon even pledged to “keep prices low,” a statement that sounds suspiciously like “caught under the White House’s roof, we have no choice but to comply.”

1.3 Service Sector in the Crosshairs: “Blanket Attack” Shocks the Market

By extending tariffs to services, the administration demolished the notion of a “safe harbor” for the service industry. This move is ruthless, absolute, and blindsided investors. Trump is firing across the entire battlefield—whether you sell widgets or streaming hours, you’re on the hook. If Netflix’s overseas shows get strangled by tariffs, subscribers could storm out in protest. And if the Trump family did load up on put options, it’s a case of “referee and gambler” at once—Trump has outplayed the market.

2. Market Sentiment Rollercoaster: Panic, Rally, and Then a “Nosedive”

U.S. stocks opened in a sea of red, all thanks to tariff fears. But at 10:00 a.m., the April ISM Services PMI hit 51.6—well above the expected 50.2 and last month’s 50.8—offering a momentary lifeline. Then Trump backpedaled, the White House chimed in, fueling a rebound… only to have intraday option unwinds slash those gains, leaving indices slightly lower at the close. Today’s sentiment swings were a “Rollercoaster Plus” ride.

2.1 ISM Data to the Rescue: U.S. Services Still Chugging Along

April’s services PMI at 51.6 proved the sector isn’t down for the count, calming nerves and sparking an initial rally.

2.2 White House Reversal: Trump Backtracks, Aides Backfill

Trump admitted the film tariff isn’t set in stone, and White House spokesperson Beson hinted that trade deals could be signed this week, with progress in talks in the coming weeks. Markets seized on the hope, powering stocks higher. Meanwhile, U.S.–Japan discussions hit a snag—Tokyo complained of doing the heavy lifting for years, only for Washington to respond, “You have no cards; we hold all the leverage.” Japan’s finance minister then rushed to “clarify” his weekend remarks, reversing course thrice in one day.

2.3 Late-Day “Crash”: Option Unwinds Erase Gains

Just as the rebound gained traction, a wave of same-day option expiries and profit-taking slammed the brakes. A humbling reminder: even the most euphoric rallies can be cut short by traders’ cold logic. If Trump really did buy Netflix puts, today’s late dip would have been a payday for him. Retail investors guess at the drama; institutions tally their actual gains and losses.

3. S&P 500’s Nine-Day Win Streak Snapped: When Expectations Overheat

The S&P 500’s nine-day winning streak ended today—one day short of tying the longest run since 1995. After rebounding from oversold to overbought, the market’s coil was wound too tight. Now everyone’s betting that trade deals will de-escalate the global tariff war. But if those pacts disappoint, reality will hand out a harsh rebound.

3.1 From “Pit” to “Ceiling”: Overshoot Risk Looms

Since April 2’s “Tariff Relief Day,” stocks have recouped losses on the premise that fresh deals will douse trade tensions. Yet trading volume has withered like autumn leaves—investors are too cautious to truly commit.

3.2 Wednesday’s Fed Decision: The Make-or-Break Moment

All eyes now turn to Wednesday’s Federal Reserve rate announcement. Corporate earnings and economic data are secondary; the Fed’s verdict will set the next course. One word can ignite a rally—or trigger a rout.

In this high-stakes atmosphere, retail traders may feel like the show’s audience while the real players call the shots. Expectations may have flown too high; if the Fed or Trump’s tariff drama delivers a “plot twist,” retail could be left nursing bruises. Netflix’s wild swings offer a clear lesson: hype can evaporate in an instant.

4. Tech Titans’ Earnings: Steady Yet Shaky Under Tariff Threats

Ninety-four S&P 500 companies report earnings this week. Tech giants have generally beaten expectations, but the sword of tariffs still dangles overhead.

4.1 The “Magnificent Seven” Hold Firm: Resilience Inspires Confidence

Aside from NVIDIA, the other six mega-caps have reported. While growth rates aren’t as explosive as last year, they’ve still outpaced forecasts. Bloomberg projects the “Mag7” will see 21.6% earnings growth and 9.7% revenue gains in 2025, providing solid ballast for the broader market.

4.2 Tariff Uncertainty Clouds Guidance: Apple Speaks Up, Others Hedge

Apple alone bluntly warned its Q2 profits could take a $900 million hit from tariffs. Google, Meta, and Microsoft mentioned macro uncertainty but stuck to optimistic guidance. Tesla refused to offer clarity (“Too uncertain to guide”), and Amazon expanded its profit range while professing loyalty to the administration. These CEOs are publicly unflappable, but everyone knows they’re on edge. If Netflix gets hit, only Trump’s secret put-option betters will sleep soundly.

5. Oil Prices Plunge 20%: Saudi Output Surge—Punishment or Gift?

Since Trump returned to the White House, oil has dropped over 20%, with the OPEC+ production ramp playing the villain. Saudi Arabia’s aggressive output is both an economic move and a geopolitical gambit.

5.1 Saudi “Flooding the Market”: 411,000 Barrels a Day Extra

OPEC+ surprised markets with a large April increase and plans to add another 411,000 barrels per day in June, with further boosts through October. Oversupply has sent prices tumbling faster than a mid-season sale.

5.2 Political Chess: Aligning with Trump or Countering Iran?

The timing of Saudi’s output hike aligns eerily with Trump’s tariff threats—are they gifting the White House lower inflation? Meanwhile, Saudi fears a revived Iran nuclear deal boosting Iranian exports. This oil-price war is as much about internal power struggles as it is global strategy.

Saudi Arabia’s move helps curb U.S. inflation, buoying airlines at the expense of oil producers. If the Trump administration is coordinating behind the scenes, Netflix puts might be small change compared to energy-market profits.

Conclusion: Market Mayhem—Spectate or Participate?

Trump’s tariff drama has thrown markets into turmoil. Netflix’s stock “dying and coming back to life” is just the tip of the iceberg. Tech giants remain resilient, oil prices are in freefall, and the Fed’s decision looms large. As retail investors, should you watch the show from the sidelines or jump in? And remember, the Trump family’s alleged Netflix put options may be the hidden wildcard in today’s biggest financial drama. The show is far from over—stay tuned!
